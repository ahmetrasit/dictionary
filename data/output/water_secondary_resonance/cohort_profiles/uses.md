# Water cohort usage baseline: `شرب`, `سقي`, `غرق`, and water-directed `ورد`

## Scope and method

This is a neutral, pre-resonance usage profile. It uses only:

- the four root packets `root_000783` (`ش ر ب`), `root_000722` (`س ق ي`), `root_001080` (`غ ر ق`), and `root_001640` (`و ر د`);
- positioned forms and Quran text reconstructed from the local QAC database;
- for contextual outcome checks, the centered five-ayah window (two ayat before and two after the focus ayah, clipped at surah boundaries).

It does **not** assign dormant branches, propose secondary readings, use tafsir, or consult prior latent-activation output. QAC occurrences are treated as root-level morphological evidence, in accordance with the packet warning that they are not branch assignments.

Counts distinguish:

- **focus ayat**: distinct ayat to be analyzed once;
- **target morphemes**: positioned same-root forms in those ayat. A focus ayah can contain more than one target morpheme.

## `شرب` family (`root_000783`)

### Eligible focus set

- **34 focus ayat; 39 target morphemes.**
- Focus refs: `2:60`, `2:93`, `2:187`, `2:249`, `2:259`, `6:70`, `7:31`, `7:160`, `10:4`, `16:10`, `16:66`, `16:69`, `18:29`, `19:26`, `23:33`, `26:155`, `35:12`, `36:73`, `37:46`, `38:42`, `38:51`, `47:15`, `52:19`, `54:28`, `56:54`, `56:55`, `56:68`, `69:24`, `76:5`, `76:6`, `76:21`, `77:43`, `78:24`, `83:28`.
- Multiple target morphemes occur in `2:60` (`مَشْرَب` and `اشربوا`), `2:249` (two forms of `شرب`), `23:33` (two forms of `يشرب`), `26:155` (two instances of `شِرْب`), and `56:55` (`شاربون` and `شُرْب`).
- Surface/lemma range in QAC: finite and imperative `شرب`, beverage noun `شراب`, drinker noun `شاربين`, allotted share `شِرْب`, act noun `شُرْب`, and place/source plural or singular `مَشْرَب/مشارب`.

### Recurring agents, actions, and roles

- The most repeated construction is a human recipient consuming a provided liquid. The consumer may be an addressed community (`2:60`, `7:31`), Mary (`19:26`), an army under test (`2:249`), people considering ordinary water (`56:68`), the people of the Garden, or the people of punishment.
- `كلوا واشربوا` is a stable pairing in `2:60`, `2:187`, `7:31`, `52:19`, `69:24`, and `77:43`. Eating and drinking jointly mark provision, but their framing varies: worldly permission with limits, or afterlife enjoyment as recompense.
- The provider is often divine or the liquid is explicitly situated within divine provision. This is overt in `2:60`, `16:10`, `16:66`, `16:69`, `19:26`, `38:42`, `56:68`, and `76:21`.
- Several nominal forms shift attention away from the act of consuming:
  - `مَشْرَب/مشارب` identifies a known access point or sources/uses (`2:60`, `7:160`, `36:73`).
  - `شِرْب` identifies an allotted turn or share (`26:155`, `54:28`).
  - `شراب` identifies the drink by source, quality, preservation, or consequence.
- The family is not limited to water. Attested liquids include rain-derived water (`16:10`), milk (`16:66`), a multicolored bee-derived drink (`16:69`), wine (`37:46`, `47:15`), hot water (`6:70`, `10:4`, `47:15`, `56:54`), and purified or blended Garden drinks (`76:5`, `76:21`, `83:28`).

### Recurring outcomes and rhetorical frames

- **Provision with a boundary:** consumption is frequently permitted but delimited. The limit can be ethical (`ولا تسرفوا`, `7:31`), temporal (`2:187`), quantitative (`2:249`), communal (`26:155`, `54:28`), or tied to non-corruption (`2:60`).
- **Disclosure of character through consumption:** at the river in `2:249`, the amount consumed separates the small obedient group from the majority before battle. In the five-ayah window, restraint precedes perseverance and victory (`2:247-251`).
- **Sign and gratitude:** drink is presented as evidence in creation or provision (`16:10`, `16:66`, `16:69`, `35:12`, `36:73`, `56:68`). These ayat repeatedly direct attention to source, transformation, palatability, differentiated waters, benefit, reflection, or gratitude.
- **Reward:** the Garden cluster presents pleasant, accessible, pure, or satisfying drink (`37:46`, `38:51`, `47:15`, `52:19`, `69:24`, `76:5-6`, `76:21`, `77:43`, `83:28`). In three ayat, `كلوا واشربوا هنيئًا` explicitly ties ease of consumption to prior deeds (`52:19`, `69:24`, `77:43`).
- **Punishment or deprivation:** the counter-cluster gives hot, harmful, or absent drink (`6:70`, `10:4`, `18:29`, the latter half of `47:15`, `56:54-55`, `78:24`). The same general consumption domain therefore supports a sharp outcome contrast based on the drink and recipient.
- **Narrative relief or restoration:** `19:26` gives Mary immediate food, drink, and composure; `38:42` gives Job a cool washing place and drink; `2:259` uses an unchanged drink alongside food and restored bones in a resurrection sign.
- **Embodied commonality:** `23:33` puts eating and drinking in the opponents' argument that the messenger is merely a human like them. Drinking is evidentiary there, not chiefly provisioning.

### Meaningful subclusters

1. **Regulated worldly consumption and water access:** `2:60`, `2:187`, `2:249`, `7:31`, `7:160`, `26:155`, `54:28`.
2. **Worldly provision, bodily benefit, and signs:** `2:259`, `16:10`, `16:66`, `16:69`, `19:26`, `35:12`, `36:73`, `38:42`, `56:68`.
3. **Afterlife reward:** `37:46`, `38:51`, `47:15`, `52:19`, `69:24`, `76:5`, `76:6`, `76:21`, `77:43`, `83:28`.
4. **Afterlife punishment or deprivation:** `6:70`, `10:4`, `18:29`, `47:15`, `56:54`, `56:55`, `78:24`.
5. **Polemical or non-literal uses:** `2:93` and `23:33`, for different reasons described below.

`47:15` intentionally belongs to both afterlife clusters because it places Garden drinks and the hot water of punishment in one explicit comparison.

### Outliers and cautions

- `2:93` is the clearest lexical outlier: `أُشْرِبُوا في قلوبهم العجل` is not physical drinking. Its local window (`2:91-95`) is about selective belief, calf attachment, covenant disobedience, and failed claims to faith. It must not be pooled statistically with literal beverage events without a separate flag.
- `23:33` is literal human drinking, but its rhetorical role is polemical classification of a messenger as an ordinary human, not the quality or provision of a drink.
- `2:259` uses `شراب` as preserved evidence in a resurrection demonstration; no consumption event occurs in the focus ayah.
- `36:73` has `مشارب` among livestock benefits without naming a specific liquid or depicting a drinking event.
- `78:24` is defined by negation: no coolness and no drink is tasted. It is a deprivation case rather than an administered harmful drink.

## `سقي` family (`root_000722`)

### Eligible focus set

- **24 focus ayat; 25 target morphemes.**
- Focus refs: `2:60`, `2:71`, `7:160`, `9:19`, `12:41`, `12:70`, `13:4`, `14:16`, `15:22`, `16:66`, `23:21`, `25:49`, `26:79`, `28:23`, `28:24`, `28:25`, `47:15`, `72:16`, `76:17`, `76:21`, `77:27`, `83:25`, `88:5`, `91:13`.
- `28:23` contains two target morphemes, `يسقون` and `نسقي`; all other focus ayat contain one.
- Surface/lemma range in QAC: direct `سقى`, causative `أسقى`, request `استسقى`, passive recipient constructions, service/institution noun `سقاية`, vessel noun `سقاية`, and watering-right noun `سقيا`.

### Recurring agents, actions, and roles

- Unlike `شرب`, this cohort normally foregrounds a **transfer relation**: a provider or source causes a recipient to receive liquid. The recurring roles are provider, liquid, and recipient; one or two may be grammatically implicit.
- God is the explicit or contextually supplied provider in the major creation/provision cluster: communal water after Moses' request (`2:60`, `7:160`), rain-derived drinking water (`15:22`), milk (`16:66`, `23:21`), rain for dead land, livestock, and people (`25:49`), Abraham's sustenance statement (`26:79`), abundant water (`72:16`), fresh water (`77:27`), and a purified Garden drink (`76:21`).
- Human providers occupy defined social roles:
  - service to pilgrims (`9:19`);
  - a cupbearer serving wine to his lord (`12:41`);
  - shepherds and two waiting women at Midian (`28:23`);
  - Moses watering for the women (`28:24-25`).
- Nonhuman recipients are normal: tilled land or crops (`2:71`, `13:4`), livestock (`25:49`), and the she-camel's protected watering (`91:13`). This cohort therefore covers irrigation and animal provision as naturally as human drinking.
- Passive forms are prominent in the afterlife cluster. The recipient is made to drink, while the moral outcome is carried by the liquid and setting rather than by the transfer verb alone (`14:16`, `47:15`, `76:17`, `83:25`, `88:5`).

### Recurring outcomes and rhetorical frames

- **Creation sign:** one water can nourish adjacent plots that yield different produce (`13:4`); rain gives life and supplies many people and animals (`25:49`); milk emerges from within cattle (`16:66`, `23:21`). The action links hidden or remote source to accessible benefit.
- **Dependence and custody:** `15:22` explicitly denies that the addressed humans store the supplied rainwater themselves. `26:79` places feeding and watering together under divine care. `72:16` makes abundant water conditional within a warning sequence.
- **Service and social ordering:** at Midian (`28:23-25`), ordinary watering practice exposes unequal access; Moses' act of watering produces an invitation, recompense, and a declaration of safety within the five-ayah window. In `9:19`, watering pilgrims is a recognized service but is explicitly denied equivalence with faith and striving.
- **Benefit/harm contrast:** the cohort is morally neutral at the transfer level. The supplied object can be fresh water, milk, wine, a pure Garden drink, boiling water, or pus-like water. The recipient's outcome is determined by the supplied substance and rhetorical setting.
- **Allocated rights and violations:** `91:13` compresses the she-camel narrative into a warning to respect `سقياها`; the next ayah records rejection, hamstringing, and punishment. The same water-right organization is also present in the `شرب` cohort at `26:155` and `54:28`.

### Meaningful subclusters

1. **Divine worldly provision and creation signs:** `2:60`, `7:160`, `13:4`, `15:22`, `16:66`, `23:21`, `25:49`, `26:79`, `72:16`, `77:27`.
2. **Human watering as work or service:** `2:71` (negated irrigation capability), `9:19`, `12:41`, `28:23`, `28:24`, `28:25`.
3. **Afterlife administration of drink:** reward in `76:17`, `76:21`, `83:25`; punishment in `14:16`, the latter half of `47:15`, and `88:5`.
4. **Watering access and protected allotment:** `2:60`, `7:160`, `28:23-25`, `91:13`.
5. **Nominal object/institution rather than an act of watering:** `9:19`, `12:70`, `91:13`.

These subclusters overlap where an ayah performs more than one role.

### Outliers and cautions

- `12:70` uses `السقاية` for the drinking vessel placed in Benjamin's baggage; the local window identifies it with the missing royal cup (`صواع الملك`, `12:72`). It is an object, not an event of supplying drink.
- `9:19` uses `سقاية الحاج` as an institution/service placed in a value comparison. Its function in the local window is status evaluation, not a report of a particular watering event.
- `2:71` is a negated capability in the cow's description (`لا ... تسقي الحرث`), not an accomplished irrigation event.
- `12:41` supplies wine to a human lord in a dream interpretation. It is occupational court service and differs from both divine provision and water access.
- `72:16` is a counterfactual/conditional statement within admonition; abundant water is not narrated as an accomplished event.
- `91:13` names a protected watering entitlement. The following violation and punishment make allocation, rather than liquid transfer, its primary discourse role.

## `غرق` family (`root_001080`)

### Eligible focus set

- **23 focus ayat; 23 target morphemes.**
- Focus refs: `2:50`, `7:64`, `7:136`, `8:54`, `10:73`, `10:90`, `11:37`, `11:43`, `17:69`, `17:103`, `18:71`, `21:77`, `23:27`, `25:37`, `26:66`, `26:120`, `29:40`, `36:43`, `37:82`, `43:55`, `44:24`, `71:25`, `79:1`.
- Surface/lemma range in QAC: causative `أغرق` forms, conditional `يغرق/نغرق`, result noun/adjective `مغرقون/مغرقين`, event noun `الغرق`, and adverbial/intensive `غرقًا`.

### Recurring agents, actions, and roles

- Completed drowning is overwhelmingly presented as divine action against a culpable collective. First-person plural divine forms (`أغرقنا/أغرقناهم`) dominate the completed-event wording.
- Two narrative populations account for most occurrences:
  - Pharaoh and his forces: `2:50`, `7:136`, `8:54`, `10:90`, `17:103`, `26:66`, `43:55`, `44:24`;
  - Noah's rejecting people: `7:64`, `10:73`, `11:37`, `11:43`, `21:77`, `23:27`, `25:37`, `26:120`, `37:82`, `71:25`.
- Drowning is frequently paired with the rescue or protected carriage of another group. The contrast is explicit in `2:50`, `7:64`, `10:73`, and in the surrounding Noah and sea-crossing narratives. The water event separates groups rather than affecting everyone indiscriminately.
- Culpability is repeatedly lexicalized near the target: denial of signs or messengers, ظلم, sin, heedlessness, aggression, or provoking divine retribution (`7:64`, `7:136`, `8:54`, `10:73`, `17:69`, `21:77`, `25:37`, `29:40`, `43:55`, `71:25`).
- Watercraft are a recurring protective or endangered space. Noah's ark preserves one group while others drown; `17:69` and `36:43` warn current sea travelers; `18:71` voices a feared drowning after a ship is damaged.

### Recurring outcomes and rhetorical frames

- **Judgment plus rescue:** the dominant frame is not a neutral accident. Drowning concludes a period of warning and rejection, while a prophet and/or believers are saved.
- **Retrospective sign:** several ayat summarize prior events as evidence or admonition (`2:50`, `8:54`, `17:103`, `21:77`, `25:37`, `29:40`). `25:37` explicitly says Noah's people were made a sign for people.
- **Foreannounced outcome:** `11:37`, `23:27`, and `44:24` label a group `مغرقون` before the completion is narrated. This gives drowning the role of settled outcome within divine instruction.
- **Moment of failed escape:** `10:90` depicts drowning reaching Pharaoh after pursuit; `11:43` places the wave between Noah and his son after the son proposes a mountain as protection. Both emphasize the collapse of a claimed means of escape.
- **Present maritime warning:** `17:69` and `36:43` are conditional, not completed drownings. Their local framing denies security and stresses absence of rescuer except divine mercy (`36:41-44`).
- **Feared but unrealized drowning:** in `18:71`, Moses infers that damaging the ship will drown its people. The local window presents this as his immediate objection, not the narrator's statement that drowning occurred.
- **Catalogue of punishments:** `29:40` places drowning alongside storm-stones, blast, and earth-collapse, with each group taken for its sin and divine injustice explicitly denied.

### Meaningful subclusters

1. **Pharaoh sea judgment:** `2:50`, `7:136`, `8:54`, `10:90`, `17:103`, `26:66`, `43:55`, `44:24` (8 ayat).
2. **Noah flood judgment:** `7:64`, `10:73`, `11:37`, `11:43`, `21:77`, `23:27`, `25:37`, `26:120`, `37:82`, `71:25` (10 ayat).
3. **Maritime possibility rather than completed communal judgment:** `17:69`, `18:71`, `36:43` (3 ayat).
4. **General punishment catalogue:** `29:40` (1 ayah).
5. **Lexically isolated intensive/oath form:** `79:1` (1 ayah).

### Outliers and cautions

- `79:1` (`والنازعات غرقًا`) contains no overt water, victim, drowning event, or rescue contrast in the available three-ayah clipped window (`79:1-3`). QAC assigns the root, but it should be separately flagged from the 22 overt drowning/submergence cases rather than assumed to depict water.
- `18:71` is an accusation about intended consequence (`لتغرق أهلها`), not an accomplished drowning and not divine punishment in the focus window.
- `17:69` and `36:43` are conditional warnings. They establish vulnerability to drowning but do not narrate its occurrence.
- `10:90` uses the event noun `الغرق` with Pharaoh as the one overtaken; it differs syntactically from the dominant divine causative verb.
- `11:37`, `23:27`, and `44:24` use result/patient forms prospectively. Their event status comes from the narrative forecast, not a completed action in the focus ayah.

## Direct water-arrival `ورد` (`root_001640`)

### Eligible focus set

- **2 focus ayat; 2 target morphemes.**
- Focus refs: `12:19` (`واردهم`) and `28:23` (`ورد ماء مدين`).
- Other Quran occurrences of the root are intentionally excluded here because this cohort is restricted to direct water-arrival roles.
- `28:23` also belongs to the `سقي` cohort and should be processed once as a focus window with separate target-word records.

### Recurring agents, actions, and roles

- Both uses involve travelers reaching a water source during a larger journey narrative.
- In `12:19`, a caravan sends its designated water-arriver/drawer. He lowers a bucket and unexpectedly retrieves Joseph.
- In `28:23`, Moses himself reaches the water of Midian. He finds a crowd watering and two women holding back until the shepherds leave.
- The water point is a social and logistical node in both cases. Arrival is immediately followed by seeing/finding a person or people, and then by practical action involving water (`أدلى دلوه`, `12:19`; `فسقى لهما`, `28:24`).

### Recurring outcomes and rhetorical frames

- **Encounter threshold:** neither ayah treats arrival at water as an endpoint. It initiates an encounter that redirects the protagonist's circumstances.
- **Hidden or disadvantaged person revealed:** the bucket reveals Joseph; Moses' arrival reveals two women excluded from immediate access by the active shepherds.
- **Rapid shift from water action to social/economic consequence:** in `12:19-21`, discovery leads to concealment as merchandise, sale, and Joseph's placement in Egypt. In `28:23-25`, Moses' watering leads to an invitation, recompense, narration of his flight, and a declaration that he has escaped the wrongdoing people.
- **Group and delegate structure:** the caravan sends `واردهم`; at Midian, a crowd already controls ordinary watering while Moses acts for the two women. Water arrival therefore takes place within group-organized access in both windows.
- **Providential narrative framing:** the local outcomes exceed the immediate aim of obtaining water. `12:21` states Joseph's establishment in the land; `28:22` asks for guidance to the right way and `28:25` declares safety after the water encounter. This is a description of the overt five-ayah sequence, not a secondary lexical claim.

### Meaningful subclusters and outliers

- With only two cases, stable subclusters would be artificial. The useful distinction is grammatical and role-based:
  - `12:19`: a **designated person noun**, the group's water-arriver/drawer;
  - `28:23`: a **finite arrival verb** whose explicit goal is `ماء مدين`.
- `12:19` never states the water noun in the focus ayah; the bucket action and the root packet's water-arriver definition establish the direct-water role. It should retain a contextual-evidence flag.
- `28:23` explicitly names the water and immediately contains two `سقي` forms, making it the denser water-access case.
