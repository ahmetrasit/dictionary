# Why the Quran chooses one water word rather than another

## Main conclusion

The water words are not simply interchangeable names for the same physical thing. They select different **relations between water and the scene**:

- **ماء** (*ma'*) presents water as a substance or material.
- **بحر** (*bahr*) presents a large water body as an extended domain.
- **يم** (*yamm*) presents it as an enclosing destination into which something is consigned.
- **موج** (*mawj*) presents water as moving, covering instability.
- The rain words distinguish falling, relief, visible emergence, force, flow, and abundance.
- **شرب**, **سقي**, and **ورد** describe the same water event from the receiver, provider, and arriver respectively.

This is the best answer to “why this word here?” The choice tells the reader which participant or phase of the water event to look at. A substitute may remain factually possible, but it would organize the scene differently.

This report describes recurring usage patterns and locally supported effects. It does not claim access to an unexpressed authorial intention, and it does not treat every root branch as active merely because it is available in a dictionary.

## The central contrast: ماء, بحر, يم, and موج

### ماء: water as material

**ماء** is the least committed about the water's shape. It can descend, enter soil, be stored, be drunk, purify, generate life, become unavailable, or punish. That flexibility is precisely why it is used when the important issue is **what the substance does or becomes**, rather than the spatial form of the water body.

In 13:17, for example, water carries foam and is compared with heated metal carrying dross. The water is a material in which surface appearance is separated from enduring benefit. In 22:5, reproductive fluid and rain-revived earth occur in one generative sequence: water is material deposited into a receptive place and made productive. In 24:39, the mirage promises water as the substance that would satisfy need, then yields nothing.

Why not **بحر** or **يم** in such scenes? Those words would introduce a bounded narrative role: an expanse to traverse or a body into which something is handed over. **ماء** leaves the substance free to circulate through sky, earth, body, plant, ritual, and punishment.

### بحر: water as an extended field

**بحر** presents water as a broad domain with an edge, interior, route, resources, and dangers. It therefore belongs naturally with ships, fishing, trade, land/sea totality, crossing, and the meeting of two seas. Those ordinary uses do not need a hidden “travel” resonance; traversability is already part of the primary viewpoint.

The Exodus scenes use **بحر** when the decisive act is opening a path through the obstacle. In 2:50 and 26:63 it is split; in 20:77 a dry road is made through it. The word lets the same object be both barrier and route. In 18:61 the fish takes a path within the sea, making its interior a sign-bearing corridor. In 18:109 and 31:27, the sea becomes ink: its physical breadth is converted into a measure of expressive supply, which still proves insufficient for the divine words.

Why not **يم**? A **بحر** can be entered and crossed while the traveler retains a path and orientation. That is the required perspective in the rescue scenes.

### يم: water as a receiving and engulfing destination

**يم** occurs only in the Moses-Pharaoh narrative complex in this corpus. It is used when a person or object is **put, thrown, or carried into an encompassing water body**, after which control passes away from the thing consigned.

This produces a precise narrative pattern:

- Infant Moses is placed into the **يم** and preserved (20:39; compare 28:7).
- The calf's remains are scattered into the **يم** as irreversible disposal (20:97).
- Pharaoh is cast into the **يم**, and his army is covered by it (20:78; 28:40; 51:40).
- Moses later receives a path through the **بحر** (20:77).

The switch in 20:77-78 is especially revealing. The same water is **بحر** while it is a traversable field opened for Moses, then **يم** when it becomes the engulfing terminus of Pharaoh's pursuit. Geography has not changed; the participant's relation to the water has.

The resulting interpretation is: **water is بحر for the one given a path through it, but يم for the one handed over to its encompassing power**. This also links Moses' infancy and mission. As an infant he is surrendered to the **يم** but returned safely; as messenger he is given mastery of a path through the **بحر**. Pharaoh enters as though it were still a traversable **بحر**, but the narrative names his outcome as **يم**.

There is a sharper reversal across the Moses cycle. In 20:39, the **يم hands Moses to Pharaoh**: it carries the casket to shore, and Pharaoh takes the child. Later, in 28:40 and 51:40, **Pharaoh is handed to the يم**: he and his forces are seized and cast into it. The first delivery places the apparently helpless child inside Pharaoh's house but preserves his future; the second removes the apparently sovereign ruler and ends his future. The calf's disposal in 20:97 extends the pattern from Pharaoh to the rival cult object. What opposes Moses' mission is eventually consigned to the same receiving water through which Moses was first preserved.

The root packet also contains an intention/aim branch, but that should not be smuggled into every **يم** occurrence. In most cases intentionality belongs to the commands and casting verbs, not to the noun. Only 20:39 provides a modest secondary effect because the **يم** itself is grammatically assigned the act of carrying the chest to shore.

### موج: water as unstable motion

**موج** selects the moving surface or mass that surrounds, separates, obscures, or mixes. It is appropriate for Noah's wave separating father and son, the wave-like mountain in the flood, layered sea darkness, and crowds surging into one another. **بحر** would name the domain; **موج** makes unstable motion itself the agent of lost visibility and lost control.

No credible secondary root branch was found here. That negative result matters: the primary meaning already explains the scenes, so no additional resonance should be invented.

## Rain is divided by how it is experienced

### مطر: what falls upon a target

**مطر** foregrounds the event as descent **onto recipients**. In the Quranic cohort it is predominantly punitive. This is why it is used for a shower that strikes a people, rather than for rain viewed as mercy or agricultural rescue.

At 46:24 the people identify the approaching cloud as something that will rain on them, but the scene corrects their hopeful classification: it is a destructive wind. The word records expected provision at the instant it becomes judgment. At 27:58 “bad was the rain” is sharpened by nearby productive sky-water: this is descent made anti-productive.

Using **غيث** would import the viewpoint of relief after need and would fight the punitive function. Using **ودق** would shift attention to rain visibly emerging from cloud structure. Using **وابل** would specify force against a substrate rather than the recipients' exposure to a descending event.

### غيث: rain as awaited relief

**غيث** presents rain from the viewpoint of those who need rescue, provision, or release from drought. In 42:28 it comes after despair and is coordinated with the spreading of mercy. The event is not merely water falling; it is timely relief that human beings cannot produce for themselves.

That is why **غيث** is unsuitable for the punitive showers named by **مطر**. The lexical viewpoint already assumes a need answered beneficially. The secondary-resonance test therefore treats “relief after distress” cautiously: it is a powerful interpretation of the word, but much of it belongs to the word's primary contextual profile rather than to a surprising second branch.

### ودق: rain seen emerging

**ودق** isolates rain as it is visibly seen coming out through clouds. Its concern is formation and appearance, not primarily benefit or punishment. In 24:43 it belongs to a sustained visual weather sequence: clouds are driven, joined, heaped, rain is seen issuing from them, and lightning nearly removes sight.

The visual structure explains the choice of **ودق**, but the eyesight danger comes most directly from lightning. The audit therefore rejects the stronger claim that the rain word itself acquires a “removing sight” meaning. The context shapes how the rain is perceived without transferring every nearby predicate onto it.

### وابل: rain as applied force

**وابل** is a heavy downpour considered through its force on what receives it. In the paired spending parables, the same heavy rain exposes two different substrates: it strips the thin covering from rock in 2:264, but doubles the yield of a well-rooted garden in 2:265.

The point is not that heavy rain is intrinsically bad. It **reveals the recipient**. This makes it the exact word for a moral test of underlying receptivity. Generic **ماء** would name the substance but lose the stress applied to the substrate; **غيث** would prejudge the event as relief.

### سيل and ثجاج: collected flow and profuse pouring

**سيل** turns attention from descent to water collected and moving through terrain. In 13:17, valleys carry torrents according to their capacity, and the flow bears surface foam away. Its interpretive work is transport and sorting.

**ثجاج** presents abundant, continuous pouring. It belongs in a descent-to-growth sequence where profusion is the point. Neither family produced a defensible hidden branch: forceful flow and copious pouring are already their direct contributions.

## Drinking is divided by participant role

### شرب: reception into the drinker

**شرب** views liquid from the receiving side: it crosses into a person or group. This makes the verb especially suitable for questions of quantity, permission, share, quality, membership, and inward effect.

At 2:249, the river test classifies the army by how much each person takes in; reception itself becomes the measure of obedience. At 16:69, the bee's varied drink carries qualities whose healing appears in human recipients. At 23:33, matched eating and drinking classify messenger and audience as members of the same human kind. At 76:5, a mixed cup is received with its infused composition.

The most surprising use is 2:93: the calf is “drunk into” their hearts. The verb does more than say that they liked the calf. It depicts an alien object becoming inwardly absorbed, while the surrounding hearing/disobedience language shows that the covenant was not inwardly received in the same way. Drinking becomes a model of selective internalization.

### سقي: provision from giver or source

**سقي** looks at the same event from the other direction: a giver, administrator, or source supplies drink to a recipient. It is therefore used for divine provision, irrigation, milk supplied from cattle, a cup administered in reward or punishment, and human watering service.

At Midian in 28:24, Moses does not merely drink; he **waters for the women**. The verb foregrounds his intervention in an unequal access system. This is not a strong hidden meaning separate from watering, but it explains exactly why **سقي** is chosen instead of **شرب**: the scene is about who can deliver access, not about Moses or the women taking liquid into themselves.

### ورد: arrival at the water system

**ورد** moves one stage earlier. It presents approaching or entering a watering place before the act of drawing or drinking. At 28:23, Moses reaches the water of Midian and enters an already ordered scene of crowds, turns, delay, and access. At 12:19, the caravan sends its designated water-arriver ahead to the well.

The Midian passage displays the three-way distinction in one scene:

- **ورد**: Moses arrives at and enters the watering situation.
- **ماء**: the water is the contested material resource.
- **سقي**: Moses performs the act that gives the women access.

Replacing all three with a generic word for water would erase the scene's social mechanics.

## Water qualities and outcomes

### فرات, أجاج, and آسن

**فرات** presents fresh water through its fitness for drinking; **أجاج** presents harsh saline or bitter water through impaired drinkability. Their pairing is relational: what matters is not an abstract chemical label but what the water permits or denies the drinker. **آسن** presents water subject to deterioration, but Paradise water is described by negating that deterioration.

These terms did not need speculative secondary branches. Their direct contrast already interprets the scene. **ماء** alone would name the shared substance while losing the bodily evaluation that separates the waters.

### حميم

**حميم** is water brought to an extreme of heat and administered as anti-refreshment. At 22:19 it is instrumentally linked to melting or rendering both the body's interior and skin. This supports a strong additional image of an active solvent, not merely a hot drink. In 56:42 it joins scorching wind and black smoke to form an entire environment that reverses every expectation of shade and refreshment.

Other root branches such as intimacy or an appointed time require strict local cues. They should not be activated simply because they share a root spelling with the hot-water word.

### غرق

**غرق** presents the result of being overwhelmed so completely that agency and escape are lost. It is used where encompassing water completes judgment or separation. It is not merely another word for entering a **بحر**: one may traverse a sea, but drowning names the failure of any path through it.

“Killing” is not a surprising secondary meaning in these passages; it is the ordinary consequence of causative drowning. The unusual 79:1 form can evoke exhaustive drawing or pulling because no water scene is present, but the stricter audit keeps the bow-like mechanical image below the highest-confidence tier because no bow or string is stated.

### بئر and انبجاس

**بئر** selects a well as vertical, constructed access to stored water. In 22:45 the abandoned well therefore contributes unused access within a ruined settlement. **انبجاس** selects water breaking out from a source; in 7:160 the source divides into twelve recognized outflows, making emergence compatible with social allocation.

These are not synonyms for **ماء**. They name the infrastructure or event by which water becomes available. No secondary root branch survived the controls, because that primary specificity already accounts for their contexts.

## The most defensible surprising readings

After the stricter audit, the clearest cases in which a five-ayah window adds something to the primary reading are:

1. **13:17 ماء**: water becomes a medium that exposes false surface and preserves useful substance.
2. **22:5 ماء**: rain participates in a paired image of deposited fluid, receptive interior, growth, and birth.
3. **77:20 ماء**: reproductive water becomes deliberately placed, protected, and timed content.
4. **18:109 and 31:27 بحر**: the sea's breadth is converted into writing material, then shown inadequate as expressive capacity.
5. **2:50; 18:61; 20:77; 26:63 بحر**: the extended obstacle becomes an interior path.
6. **2:93 شرب**: drinking becomes selective inward incorporation, contrasted with failed reception of the covenant.
7. **2:249 شرب**: quantity received into the body becomes the exact measure separating obedience from excess.
8. **16:69 شرب**: drink carries differentiated qualities into recipients and produces healing.
9. **22:19 حميم**: hot water acts as a solvent crossing the body's outer/inner boundary.
10. **46:24 مطر**: the people's word for hoped-for rain preserves their tragic misreading of approaching destruction.
11. **2:264 وابل**: heavy rain physically enacts exposure of a morally unsupported surface.
12. **28:23 ورد**: arrival at water means entry into an ordered and unequal access system.

Some attractive readings were deliberately downgraded. The mirage at 24:39 supports false appearance, but not specifically a metallic “coating” branch. The rain at 24:43 belongs to a visual display, but lightning is what threatens sight. The **يم** passages contain deliberate commands, but intention usually belongs to the actors and verbs, not to **يم** itself. These exclusions make the positive findings more meaningful.

## Final interpretive model

The vocabulary can be read as a sequence of questions:

| Question asked by the scene | Selected word |
|---|---|
| What is the water as usable, generative, purifying, or punitive material? | ماء |
| What kind of domain, boundary, or route is this water body? | بحر |
| Into what encompassing destination is the object or person surrendered? | يم |
| What moving mass removes stability or visibility? | موج |
| What is falling onto these recipients? | مطر |
| How does needed relief arrive? | غيث |
| What rain is visibly issuing from the cloud? | ودق |
| What force tests the receiving ground? | وابل |
| What collected flow moves through terrain? | سيل |
| What is being poured in abundance? | ثجاج |
| What enters and affects the recipient? | شرب |
| Who or what supplies the recipient? | سقي |
| Who arrives at the water and enters its access order? | ورد |
| Is the water drinkable, harsh, or deteriorating? | فرات / أجاج / آسن |
| What heated anti-drink transforms the body? | حميم |
| What overwhelms beyond recovery? | غرق |
| What structure or rupture gives access to stored water? | بئر / انبجاس |

The surprising readings are strongest when the five-ayah window intensifies one of these viewpoints or converts it into another domain without erasing the ordinary meaning. They are weakest when an analyst merely notices a nearby idea and assigns it to the water word. That distinction is the central methodological result of the study.
